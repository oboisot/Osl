Detailed Description

Class to manage Ellipsoid of revolution for geographic applications.

This class defines the reference Ellipsoid of revolution (also called Spheroid) to be used for geographic applications. The Ellipsoid is generally defined from one of the two following couples:

  • \((a,f)\) \(\rightarrow\) (equatorial radius and flattening)
  • \((a,b)\) \(\rightarrow\) (equatorial radius and polar radius)

◆ getFirstFlattening()

double Osl::Geography::Ellipsoid::getFirstFlattening ( ) const

Get the first flattening parameter \(f\) of the ellipsoid.

The first flattening parameter \(f\) is related to the ellipsoid of revolution radii through the relationship:

\[ f=\dfrac{a-b}{a} \]

If \(f<0\) the ellipsoid is called oblate (as for the terrestrial ellipsoids), if \(f>0\) it is called prolate and, in the case where \(f=0\) the ellipsoid becomes a sphere.

◆ getQuarterMeridianDistance()

double Osl::Geography::Ellipsoid::getQuarterMeridianDistance ( ) const

Get the quarter meridian distance \(m_p\) of the ellipsoid.

The quarter meridian distance is defined as the distance from the equator to the pole. It is thus defined as the meridian distance (see Ellipsoid::meridianDistance) evaluated for a latitude of \(\pi/2\):

\[ m_p=m(\dfrac{\pi}{2})=aE(e) \]

and can be expressed as function of the Legendre's complete elliptic integral of second kind [DLMF]:

\[ E(k)=\int\limits_0^{\pi/2} \sqrt{1-k^2\sin^2\theta}d\theta \]

Compute the meridian distance.

The meridian distance is the distance measured along a meridian from equator to a geodetic latitude \(\phi\) and is expressed as:

\[ m(\phi)=\int\limits_0^{\phi} \dfrac{a(1-e^2)}{(1-e^2\sin^2(t))^{3/2}}dt \]

This expression can be written as function of the Legendre's incomplete elliptic integral of the third kind \(\Pi(\phi,\nu,k)\):

\[ m(\phi)=a(1-e^2)\Pi(\phi,e^2,e) \]

with the Legendre's incomplete elliptic integral being defined by [DLMF]:

\[ \Pi(\phi,\alpha^2,k)=\int\limits_0^{\phi}\dfrac{dt} {(1-\alpha^2\sin^2(t))\sqrt{1-k^2\sin^2(t)}} \]

Curvature Radius.

Compute the curvature radius of a point on the ellispoid at a given geodetic latitude \(\phi\) orientated following a given azimut \(\alpha\):

\[ \dfrac{1}{R(\phi,\alpha)}= \dfrac{\cos^2(\alpha)}{\rho(\phi)}+\dfrac{\sin^2(\alpha)}{\nu(\phi)} \]

where \(\rho(\phi)\) and \(\nu(\phi)\) are the meridian curvature radius and the prime vertical curvature radius of the ellipsoid at latitude \(\phi\), respectively.

Transform geodetic coordinates to geocentric (ECEF) coordinates.

Transform geodetic coordinates \((\lambda,\phi,H)\) to geocentric coordinates \((X,Y,Z)\), also knwon as the Earth-Centered Earth-Fixed (ECEF) cartesian coordinates system [Wiki]. The transformation is straightforward from the definition of the ellipsoid:

\[ \left\{\begin{array}{rcl} X & = & \big(\nu(\phi) + H\big)\cos(\phi)\cos(\lambda) \\ Y & = & \big(\nu(\phi) + H\big)\cos(\phi)\sin(\lambda) \\ Z & = & \big((1-e^2)\nu(\phi) + H\big)\sin(\phi) \end{array}\right. \]

where \(\nu(\phi)\) is the prime vertical curvature radius of the point at latitude \(\phi\) (see Ellipsoid::primeVerticalCurvatureRadius).

Transform geocentric (ECEF) coordinates to geodetic coordinates.

Transforms geocentric coordinates \((X,Y,Z)\) to geodetic coordinates \((\lambda,\phi,H)\) on an ellipsoid of revolution. The computation is made as follows:

  1. Longitude \(\lambda\) is computed analytically:

    \[ \lambda=\arctan\left(\frac{Y}{X}\right) \]

  2. Latitude \(\phi\) is computed from:

    1. the approximated Bowring's formula [1], [4] :

    \[ \left\{\begin{array}{rcl} u & = & \arctan\left[\dfrac{Z}{\sqrt{X^2+Y^2}}\left(1-f+ \dfrac{ae^2}{\sqrt{X^2+Y^2+Z^2}}\right)\right] \\ \phi & = & \arctan\left[\dfrac{(1-f)Z+ae^2\sin^3(u)} {(1-f)\left(\sqrt{X^2+Y^2}-ae^2\cos^3(u)\right)}\right] \end{array}\right. \]

    This formula provides an accuracy on distances of the order of m with the WGS84 Ellipsod.

    1. the computed approximated value can then be improved numerically from iterative method, providing an accurate initial guess. The method used here is the Fixed-Point Iteration method [Wikipedia] by solving the iterative scheme:

    \[ \phi_{n+1} = \arctan\left[\dfrac{Z+e^2\nu(\phi_n)\sin(\phi_n)} {\sqrt{X^2+Y^2}}\right],\quad n=0,1,2,3,\ldots \]

    until the defined numerical precision (here the machine epsilon for double precision floating point type). Initialization of this iterative method with the approximated Bowring's formula provides a very fast convergence of the iterative scheme (generally, one or two iterations are sufficient).

  3. Finally, from longitude and latitude computed previously, altitude is computed from the modified Bowring's expression [1] :

    \[ H=\sqrt{X^2+Y^2}\cos(\phi)+Z\sin(\phi)-a\sqrt{1-e^2\sin^2(\phi)} \]
