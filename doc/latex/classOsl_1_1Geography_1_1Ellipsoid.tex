\doxysubsection{Detailed Description}
Class to manage \mbox{\hyperlink{classOsl_1_1Geography_1_1Ellipsoid}{Ellipsoid}} of revolution for geographic applications. 

\DoxyHorRuler{0}
 This class defines the reference \mbox{\hyperlink{classOsl_1_1Geography_1_1Ellipsoid}{Ellipsoid}} of revolution (also called Spheroid) to be used for geographic applications. The \mbox{\hyperlink{classOsl_1_1Geography_1_1Ellipsoid}{Ellipsoid}} is generally defined from one of the two following couples\+:


\begin{DoxyItemize}
\item $(a,f)$ $\rightarrow$ (equatorial radius and flattening)
\item $(a,b)$ $\rightarrow$ (equatorial radius and polar radius)
\end{DoxyItemize}

\[ f=\dfrac{a-b}{a} \]

Get the quarter meridian distance $m_p$ of the ellipsoid. 

\DoxyHorRuler{0}
 The quarter meridian distance is defined as the distance from the equator to the pole. It is thus defined as the meridian distance (see \mbox{\hyperlink{classOsl_1_1Geography_1_1Ellipsoid_ac06a1c3f89525f5cfa2ea77b4d781209}{Ellipsoid\+::meridian\+Distance}}) evaluated for a latitude of $\pi/2$\+:

\[ m_p=m(\dfrac{\pi}{2})=aE(e) \]

and can be expressed as function of the Legendre\textquotesingle{}s complete elliptic integral of second kind \href{https://dlmf.nist.gov/19.2\#ii}{\texttt{ \mbox{[}D\+L\+MF\mbox{]}}}\+:

\[ E(k)=\int\limits_0^{\pi/2} \sqrt{1-k^2\sin^2\theta}d\theta \]

Compute the meridian distance. 

\DoxyHorRuler{0}
 The meridian distance is the distance measured along a meridian from equator to a geodetic latitude $\phi$ and is expressed as\+:

\[ m(\phi)=\int\limits_0^{\phi} \dfrac{a(1-e^2)}{(1-e^2\sin^2(t))^{3/2}}dt \]

This expression can be written as function of the Legendre\textquotesingle{}s incomplete elliptic integral of the third kind $\Pi(\phi,\nu,k)$\+:

\[ m(\phi)=a(1-e^2)\Pi(\phi,e^2,e) \]

with the Legendre\textquotesingle{}s incomplete elliptic integral being defined by \href{https://dlmf.nist.gov/19.2\#ii}{\texttt{ \mbox{[}D\+L\+MF\mbox{]}}}\+:

\[ \Pi(\phi,\alpha^2,k)=\int\limits_0^{\phi}\dfrac{dt} {(1-\alpha^2\sin^2(t))\sqrt{1-k^2\sin^2(t)}} \]

 Transform geodetic coordinates $(\lambda,\phi,H)$ to geocentric coordinates $(X,Y,Z)$, also knwon as the Earth-\/\+Centered Earth-\/\+Fixed (E\+C\+EF) cartesian coordinates system \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECEF}{\texttt{ \mbox{[}Wiki\mbox{]}}}. The transformation is straightforward from the definition of the ellipsoid\+:

\[ \left\{\begin{array}{rcl} X & = & \big(\nu(\phi) + H\big)\cos(\phi)\cos(\lambda) \\ Y & = & \big(\nu(\phi) + H\big)\cos(\phi)\sin(\lambda) \\ Z & = & \big((1-e^2)\nu(\phi) + H\big)\sin(\phi) \end{array}\right. \]

where $\nu(\phi)$ is the prime vertical curvature radius of the point at latitude $\phi$ (see \mbox{\hyperlink{classOsl_1_1Geography_1_1Ellipsoid_a5c82a35189ac6f794211637058e507b6}{Ellipsoid\+::prime\+Vertical\+Curvature\+Radius}}).

Transform geocentric (E\+C\+EF) coordinates to geodetic coordinates. 

\DoxyHorRuler{0}
 Transforms geocentric coordinates $(X,Y,Z)$ to geodetic coordinates $(\lambda,\phi,H)$ on an ellipsoid of revolution. The computation is made as follows\+:


\begin{DoxyEnumerate}
\item Longitude $\lambda$ is computed analytically\+:

\[ \lambda=\arctan\left(\frac{Y}{X}\right) \]
\item Latitude $\phi$ is computed from\+:
\begin{DoxyEnumerate}
\item the approximated Bowring\textquotesingle{}s formula \cite{Bowring_85}, \cite{IGN_TCG} \+:
\end{DoxyEnumerate}

\[ \left\{\begin{array}{rcl} u & = & \arctan\left[\dfrac{Z}{\sqrt{X^2+Y^2}}\left(1-f+ \dfrac{ae^2}{\sqrt{X^2+Y^2+Z^2}}\right)\right] \\ \phi & = & \arctan\left[\dfrac{(1-f)Z+ae^2\sin^3(u)} {(1-f)\left(\sqrt{X^2+Y^2}-ae^2\cos^3(u)\right)}\right] \end{array}\right. \]

This formula provides an accuracy on distances of the order of µm with the W\+G\+S84 Ellipsoïd.
\begin{DoxyEnumerate}
\item the computed approximated value can then be improved numerically from iterative method, providing an accurate initial guess. The method used here is the Fixed-\/\+Point Iteration method \cite{WIKI_Fixed_Point_Iteration} by solving the iterative scheme\+:
\end{DoxyEnumerate}

\[ \phi_{n+1} = \arctan\left[\dfrac{Z+e^2\nu(\phi_n)\sin(\phi_n)} {\sqrt{X^2+Y^2}}\right],\quad n=0,1,2,3,\ldots \]

until the defined numerical precision (here the machine epsilon for double precision floating point type). Initialization of this iterative method with the approximated Bowring\textquotesingle{}s formula provides a very fast convergence of the iterative scheme (generally, one or two iterations are sufficient).
\item Finally, from longitude and latitude computed previously, altitude is computed from the modified Bowring\textquotesingle{}s expression \cite{Bowring_85} \+:

\[ H=\sqrt{X^2+Y^2}\cos(\phi)+Z\sin(\phi)-a\sqrt{1-e^2\sin^2(\phi)} \]
\end{DoxyEnumerate}
